Building a website or application today is infinitely faster than when I first started in software. With tools like GPT, Gemini, and Claude, **it’s possible to take an idea off the ground and have a functional prototype in minutes.**

You can test concepts and even build an MVP in the same time it used to take just to configure a server a few years ago. For anyone working with innovation, this speed is a gift — a **steroid-enhanced version of the “Move Fast, Break Things” philosophy**, the old Facebook startup motto.

Today, it’s possible to test ideas cheaply, fail fast, and learn fast — especially **with platforms like n8n, Zapier, and Make**.

But this speed also hides a risk:  
the WOW factor generated by an impressive demo can be misleading.

A beautiful interface, an “intelligent” user flow, and AI responses that feel like science fiction **in a demo can be a trap**, leading entrepreneurs and investors to believe there’s a solid business behind it when, in practice, there isn’t a relevant problem or a sustainable model.

---

## When technology becomes the product

The lower cost of building software seems to push many entrepreneurs to focus on potential and presentation rather than on the business plan. The classic Business Model Canvas gets left in the corner of the room, gathering dust.

The **Humane case** is one of the biggest examples of this phenomenon.

The AI Pin was announced as the future of personal computing: a wearable, screenless device controlled by voice and gestures, using AI to answer questions, translate, and interact with the world.

The hype was massive, with global coverage and more than **$200 million in funding**. But in the market, the product met silence: underwhelming performance, limited real-world value, and a high price.

The result: weak sales and, less than a year later, **the startup was sold to HP for a fraction of what it had raised.**

This case illustrates a common trap in the tech world:  
**confusing initial impression with market validation.**

---

## The demo trap

An impressive demo proves only one thing: that something can be made to work. It **does not prove that someone will pay for it.**

This difficulty in separating perceived value from real value also appears in technical evaluation. MIT Technology Review Brazil has already pointed out **how AI benchmarks create a false sense of readiness**: models that shine in controlled tests may fail in the real world, where data is imperfect and context constantly changes.

We see the same problem with the **WOW factor in companies that are essentially “AI wrappers.”**

Many startups in the space are limited to sophisticated interfaces built on top of third-party APIs like GPT-4 (now GPT-5) or Claude. The risk? Extreme dependency on vendors, API costs that erode margins, and business models that are easily replicable.

It’s the same phenomenon on the commercial side:  
**an internal metric (the demo’s “WOW”) does not guarantee market success.**

---

## The opposite path: start with the problem

The contrast is clear in the case of Solinftec, for example — a Brazilian agtech that spent years understanding real agricultural pain points before investing heavily in AI.

The result was a robotics division with computer vision capable of detecting pests and reducing pesticide usage by up to 95% on farms.

**Success came from the alignment between an urgent problem, an effective solution, and a market willing to pay**, not from a flashy demo.

---

## The lesson we learned in practice

At my company, we’ve been through this ourselves. We built solutions that looked impressive on screen but failed to gain traction — not due to lack of technology, but due to a lack of clarity about the problem we were solving and its relevance to the paying customer.

We learned to **use the speed of AI to validate hypotheses** in a brutally honest way before scaling.

Before committing months of development and thousands of dollars, four questions are worth answering:

1. **Urgency** – Does this problem keep the buyer up at night?  
2. **Market** – Is there a large enough customer base with this pain?  
3. **Frequency** – Is it recurring enough to justify continuous payment?  
4. **Barriers** – What makes my business unique? Is it easy to replicate?  

If any answer is “I don’t know,” the next step is not coding — it’s investigation. Take out the Business Model Canvas, dust it off, and rethink your idea and how to validate it.

AI can accelerate the path to success — but also to failure.

The WOW factor is useful for opening doors, but sustainable ROI always starts in the same place: a real, validated, and relevant problem. Technology is the lever, but it only works when it stands on solid ground.
