Every time a new technology hype emerges, the story repeats itself. Someone from senior management throws out a generic statement like:

- “We need AI in the company.”  
- “Can we use blockchain for something?”  
- “We should be working with Big Data.”  

The problem is not the technology itself. The problem is the **void that usually comes with this kind of request.**

Companies don’t need AI, blockchain, or Big Data. Companies need to solve **real problems**: improve efficiency, reduce costs, increase revenue, mitigate risks, and deliver more value to customers.

Technology only makes sense when it is the consequence of a well-defined problem — **not the other way around.**

---

## When technology becomes the goal

When technology becomes the end goal — used just to say it’s being adopted or to signal innovation — two types of damage almost always follow.

A financial one: resources are consumed in long, expensive, and complex projects that ultimately **fail to deliver real impact.** The company now “has the technology,” but doesn’t really know **what for.**

And a human one — often even more severe: technical teams build sophisticated, well-designed solutions with solid engineering that end up shelved. Nothing is more demotivating for a tech team than seeing months of work turn into: a nice slide, a forgotten demo, a product no one uses.

For any developer, few things are as frustrating as realizing that **no one is using the system they built.**

These are classic symptoms of treating technology as an end, not a means.

---

## When the hype comes from within

There is also the reverse movement, equally dangerous.

Managers or technical leaders fall in love with a specific technology and start **forcing problems to justify it.** Common phrases include:

- “We need real-time data with Kafka.”  
- “Let’s use Kubernetes for our containers.”  
- “This case requires advanced machine learning.”  

In practice, many of these problems could be solved with much simpler solutions. Sometimes even with a well-defined business rule, a process adjustment, or a more straightforward architecture — like a simple SQS queue or a micro EC2 instance.

Complex technology comes with costs: operational cost, cognitive load, maintenance overhead, and additional risk.

Using it unnecessarily **is not innovation.** It’s irresponsibility.

---

## The role of the CTO

There is nothing wrong with experimenting, learning, or evolving technically. The mistake is **subordinating the business to technological ego**, instead of the other way around.

One of the most important roles of a CTO — especially in growing companies — is to **translate hype into rational decisions.**

That means understanding the cause before discussing the solution.

Interestingly, the more mature an organization becomes in technology, the less it talks about technology — and the more it talks about **impact.**

**Because hype fades. Problems remain.**

---

## The technology of the moment always changes

Every generation has its “technology of the moment.” It used to be **Big Data**, then **blockchain**, now **generative AI**. Tomorrow, it will be something else.

Successful companies don’t chase trendy technologies. They chase **clarity**.

Because when the problem is well defined, **the right technology naturally follows.**

---

## The question that really matters

At the end of the day, the question shouldn’t be:  
“How do we use this technology?”

But rather:  
**“What problem are we solving?”**  
And preferably: **“What is the simplest, most efficient, and most sustainable way to solve it?”**

Everything else is noise.
