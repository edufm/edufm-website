Last month, OpenAI drew attention by releasing *open-weight* GPT models. It was the first time since GPT-2, back in 2019, that the company made one of its models freely available.

This time, it came with an even more reassuring license for those who want to use the technology: **Apache 2.0**. This license allows unrestricted commercial use, modifications, and includes an explicit patent grant. In practice, even large companies can use these models without fear of potential litigation.

For many, the decision seemed contradictory. Why release for free a technology that cost billions of dollars in research and development?

The answer may lie in a less intuitive logic, but one that has already been proven across industries: **sometimes, giving up exclusivity accelerates market growth more than keeping everything tightly protected.**

The idea is simple: sacrifice a portion of the pie to secure a slice of a **much larger pie**.

---

## The Tesla precedent

A similar move happened in 2014. That year, Tesla announced it would not sue any company using its electric vehicle patents.

At the time, the decision seemed risky, but it had a clear purpose: Elon Musk alone would not be able to transform the automotive industry.

It was necessary to foster an entire ecosystem — from increasing public adoption to enabling subsidies for charging infrastructure. By relaxing its protection, Tesla helped accelerate the adoption of electric vehicles and, in the process, positioned itself as a market leader.

---

## The OpenAI case

OpenAI is following a similar path, but with an important difference. While Tesla needed to stimulate physical infrastructure and manufacturing partners, OpenAI needs to demonstrate, in practice, the usefulness of its models.

Releasing open versions allows **developers and companies to build real solutions on top of the technology**, multiplying use cases and expanding the AI ecosystem.

This move brings more than just technical benefits. There is also a significant gain in **reputation and brand positioning**.

When a company opens its code, models, or patents, the entire market pays attention. Media coverage grows, and the company’s image becomes associated with **leadership**.

This reputational effect strengthens trust among customers, partners, and investors — an intangible asset that can be just as valuable as the technology itself.

---

## The power of open ecosystems

For decades, we’ve seen how openness creates value.

**Linux** became ubiquitous in servers and embedded systems because it was open, allowing thousands of developers to contribute and companies to build on top of it.

**Android** dominated the mobile market for the same reason. By being open, it attracted manufacturers, developers, and ultimately, users.

In OpenAI’s case, releasing free models plays a similar role. Startups and researchers can experiment without cost barriers, creating innovative applications that indirectly validate and promote GPT.

Each new solution built on top of this foundation expands the market and increases the brand’s relevance.

---

## The freemium model applied to AI

There is also an important strategic component: the **freemium model**.

Just as the free version of ChatGPT attracts millions of users who later migrate to the paid version, *open-weight* models act as an entry point.

Developers start by using open versions — powerful, but not cutting-edge. As their applications mature, the need for higher performance and support arises. At that point, many naturally migrate to paid APIs and more advanced models.

In other words, **open weight is not an act of altruism.**

It is a smart **customer acquisition strategy**, where the free initial experience demonstrates the technology’s potential and lays the groundwork for future monetization.

---

## Opening to lead

The key takeaway is that overprotection can suffocate the growth of an emerging market. Opening up, on the other hand, can position you as a market leader and accelerate the development of your entire sector.

The key is finding the right balance: share enough to catalyze the market, without losing your edge. Whether in electric vehicles, operating systems, or artificial intelligence, the lesson is the same:

**Sometimes, the safest path to leadership is not building walls — it’s opening doors.**
